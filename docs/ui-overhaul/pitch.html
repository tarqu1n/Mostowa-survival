01 · Where it hurts

What the current UI gets wrong

From an audit of src/scenes/hud/* and src/ui/* — a HUD that grew widget-by-widget and never got a layout system. Six things stand out.

Readability

8–11px monospace, everywhere

HP/food bars at 8px, hints at 8–9px, zoom buttons 24×24 — well below the ~44px / 16px-on-screen a phone needs. All text, no icons.

Layout

Hard-coded pixels, no reflow

Every widget computes x/y off fixed 360×640 constants. No safe-area insets, no landscape, no adapting beyond letterboxing.

Clutter

Corners stack by magic numbers

BUILD→CANCEL→ITEMS→DEMOLISH are manually offset; adding a button re-tunes its neighbours. A dead SPELL slot already sits on screen.

System

No design tokens

One font size, a colour set, every gap/pad/size a local literal. "Retune the whole look" isn't really possible today.

Scale

Nowhere to put depth

Today there are 3 buildables and no spells. With dozens of buildings/spells/weapons coming, there's no menu structure that holds them.

Interaction

Inventory is look-only

The slot grid can't select, drag, or equip (deferred). No hotbar for quick-swapping a weapon or downing a potion mid-fight.

02 · The field

What good phone survival UIs do

Distilled from Don't Starve: Pocket Edition, Kingdom Two Crowns, Last Day on Earth, The Long Dark and Whiteout Survival, plus the mobile-UX literature. Ten principles behind the designs.

  1. 01Two layouts, not one rotation.Portrait = one thumb from a bottom corner. Landscape = move-left / act-right like a gamepad. Offer a left/right-hand mirror.
  2. 02Interactive lives in the bottom 30–40%.On a modern 6.5″ phone only the bottom half is comfortably one-hand reachable. Passive info up top.
  3. 03≥44–48px hit targets, decoupled from art.A 24px pixel icon carries an invisible 44px hit rectangle. Biggest single lever for a pixel game.
  4. 04World and UI are separate layers.World at integer nearest-neighbour zoom; UI baked crisp at a device scale so buttons stay tap-sized.
  5. 05Respect the notch.Full-bleed world behind the Dynamic Island; inset controls to the safe rectangle. Free in CSS, painful in Phaser.
  6. 06Meters show state + trend, not numbers.Circular icon-meters. Persist only health, hunger, day/night; fade the rest in on change.
  7. 07The day/night dial is the spine.Calm by day; surface "night is coming" at dusk; promote wave number + enemies-left at night.
  8. 08Build = ghost + snap + confirm.Never place on raw touch-up. Ghost snaps green/red → nudge → ✔/⟳/cancel → "place again" for wall runs.
  9. 09Loadout on the quick surface, catalog behind it.Assign a few favourites to a hotbar/wheel for one-tap use; browse the full set in a categorised grid. (See §03.)
  10. 10Acknowledge every tap; reserve red.Micro-scale/flash on press, juice key moments (floaty "+5 wood", shake, particles), keep red for danger only.

03 · Depth without clutter

Loadout vs. catalog — the two-tier model

You flagged the real constraint: there'll be a lot of buildings, and lots of spells/attacks available at once. A radial wheel or a single tray tops out around 6–8 items — so every design here uses two tiers, and this is where the hotbar earns its place.

Tier 1 — Loadout (fast, few)

Always one thumb away, one tap to fire. This is your hotbar and, in Twin Grip, the action-wheel petals. Holds a handful of assigned go-tos — the axe, the bow, a Firebolt, a bandage, the wall you're spamming.

Mixed content: weapons, consumables, and spells share the same slots. Long-press a slot to reassign from the catalog.

Tier 2 — Catalog (deep, browsable)

Everything you own, in a categorised, scrollable grid — the build list (Defense / Survival / Production), the spellbook, the full pack. Opened only when you're choosing, not while you're acting.

Pick to place/equip now; pin to send it to the loadout for next time. The catalog scales to hundreds; the loadout never grows past a thumb's reach.

The mockups below are populated with placeholder content — ~19 buildings across three categories, 8 spells, 6 weapons/consumables on the hotbar — so you can judge how each direction handles the volume, not just the happy-path of three buttons.

05 · The build call

Author the menus in HTML, not Phaser

Your instinct is right, and there's precedent: the Map Builder already runs React + Tailwind v4 + shadcn/ui, deliberately kept out of the game bundle. Recommendation — a DOM overlay for the HUD and menus, with the world and in-world markers staying in Phaser. This matters more now: scrollable catalog grids, drag-to-pin, and safe-area layout are things the browser gives for free.

Recommendation

Hybrid: a DOM/React HUD layer over the Phaser canvas

DOM / React owns

  • HUD bars, meters, day/night dial, resource counts
  • Hotbar, build catalog, spellbook, pack, inspect card, companion & pause menus
  • Scrollable grids, drag-to-pin, tabs — reusing the editor's shadcn primitives & Tailwind @theme tokens
  • Safe-area insets, responsive layout, focus/accessibility

Phaser keeps

  • World, camera, entities, lighting — untouched
  • In-world markers: build ghost + grid snap, target outline, floating combat/gather text, monster HP bars, queue markers
  • Gesture mechanics on the canvas (tap / long-press paint / pan / pinch), and the twin-thumb move/aim input

Costs: a thin event bridge (game.events ↔ React state), coordinate mapping for the few DOM→world actions, and Tailwind loading on the game page (scoped; the editor proves it). The alternative — hand-rolling flexbox, safe-areas, scrollable grids and a token system in Phaser — is the exact debt §01 is made of.

06 · The pitch

Three directions to choose from

Same game, same control set, three philosophies. Every phone is live: tap the chips to move through Scavenge · Build · Fight · Spells · Night — the hotbar is present in play, and Build & Spells open the populated catalogs so you can see the volume. Ordered with your favourite first.

Direction C · your front-runner

Twin Grip

"Two thumbs, always on the sticks."

The gamepad never leaves: left corner is the move ring, right corner is a live action wheel whose petals are your loadout — equipped attacks and spells, one flick each. A slim hotbar rides the bottom edge between the thumbs for weapons/items/spells. The wheel's centre opens the deep catalog (spellbook, build list) as a grid above the still-visible gamepad. Console-feel, landscape-native, most screen for the world.

Lineage: console twin-stick · radial action games · Diablo Immortal loadout

Strengths
  • Gamepad always ready — fastest in a fight
  • Loadout + catalog scales cleanly
  • Landscape-native; maximises world
Risks
  • Highest learning curve
  • Wheel petals hide labels until learned
  • Most to build & tune (radial + catalog)

Direction B · safe / legible

Field Kit

"One bar that becomes whatever the moment needs."

A persistent bottom command bar that morphs by mode, with the hotbar riding just above it at all times. Scavenge shows Build/Pack/Craft/Status; Build swaps to the catalog + confirm cluster; Fight swaps to a move pad + attack/bow/cast pulled from your hotbar. Deep menus are tabbed bottom-sheet drawers. The most discoverable, most labelled — everything has a word on it.

Lineage: Don't Starve: Pocket Edition · Last Day on Earth

Strengths
  • Everything discoverable & labelled
  • One consistent thumb zone + hotbar
  • Gentlest learning curve
Risks
  • Bar + hotbar cost ~22% of screen
  • Least "novel"-feeling
  • Slower than a live wheel in a fight

Direction A · minimal

Emberlight

"Trust the world. Show almost nothing."

Diegetic-first — the screen is nearly all game. Meters are small rings that fade when calm; the day/night dial is the one permanent fixture. A slim auto-hiding hotbar sits low-centre for quick-swap, and everything deep (build catalog, spellbook, pack) rises as a full bottom sheet then gets out of the way. Gorgeous, but leans hardest on the player to remember what's where.

Lineage: Kingdom Two Crowns · The Long Dark

Strengths

  • Hotbar keeps quick actions without chrome
Risks

  • No always-on gamepad — slower to act
  • Deep menus feel like a context switch

07 · Deciding

How I'd take this forward

Given your steer, I'd build Twin Grip (C): the always-open gamepad is the thing you liked, and the loadout-wheel + catalog split is the cleanest answer to "lots of buildings and spells". Borrow Field Kit's labelled catalog grids wholesale (they're the same DOM component), and keep a Field Kit fallback layout in your pocket as the accessible/low-dexterity option — same data, calmer surface. The hotbar is common to all three, so it's worth building first whichever way you lean.

Next step once you pick: a plan-feature pass — event bridge → token system → hotbar + catalog components → the wheel/bar surface → per-mode wiring. A few things to settle first:

Q1Confirm Twin Grip as the base (with Field Kit catalogs + fallback), or a different blend?
Q2Wheel size — how many petals before the catalog takes over? (I'd cap at 6 + centre.)
Q3Hotbar length & assignment — 5 or 6 slots? Manual pin only, or auto-populate recent?
Q4Real building/spell lists — the mockups use placeholders; what's the actual planned roster so the catalog categories are right?
Q5Portrait-only first, or portrait + landscape from the start (Twin Grip is built for both)?
